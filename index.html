Fabrics of the world is a Pirate Technics project for the Festival of the World exhibited at Southbank Centre throughout Summer 2012.
The Baobab tree is made from fabrics around the world, of which 40% are designed and selected by 31 international Masters students studying Textiles at Chelsea Collage of Art and Design.

Afghan Silk

Silk from Afghanistan - a reminder of ancient silk trade routes
Under the Baobab on Flickr
Afghanistan sits at the heart of Central Asia, at the meeting point of ancient trade routes - known as ‘The Silk Road’. From here routes led east to China; some north to the great cities of Bokhara, Samarkand and Khiva; some south-east to India; others east to Iran, which intern continued to the Mediterranean sea and Europe. Goods en route to any of these places had to pass through Afghanistan. Cities strategically placed on these trade routes, were able to benefit hugely from this trade.

Batik

Batik dyeing is an ancient art form. Discoveries show it already existed in Egypt in the 4th century BC, where it was used to wrap mummies; linen was soaked in wax, and scratched using a sharp tool.
Under the Baobab on Flickr
In Asia, the technique was practiced in China during the T’ang Dynasty (618-907 BC), and in India and Japan during the Nara Period (645-794 BC).
Pirate Technics on Flickr
In Africa it was originally practiced by the Yoruba tribe in Nigeria, and the Soninke an Wolof tribes of Senegal.

Marocco

Since the conversion of the Nomadic Berber tribes to Islam in the 8th and 9th centuries and with it’s strong geographical importance, Morocco has had a very chequered history, culminating in it’s independence from France in 1956.
Under the Baobab on Flickr
Throughout the centuries culture, art and craftsmanship has flourished. With the Atlas and Rif mountain ranges and fertile river valleys, agriculture, especially the breeding of sheep and goats for wool had developed. The weaving of rugs, kilims and textiles being a natural extension of this, playing a major role in domestic life and commerce.

Keffiyeh

Traditionally worn by Palestinian farmers, the Keffiyeh was worn by Palestinian men of every rank. It became a symbol of Palestinian nationalism during the Arab Revolt of the 1930s. Its prominence increased in the 1960s with the beginning of the Palestinian resistance movement and its adoption by Palestinian politician Yasser Arafat.

Kekfesto

Kekfesto Cotton - Hungarian Blue Print Fabric
Under the Baobab on Flickr
It was mostly used in rural areas among farmers and provided a basis for folk dresses.
Under the Baobab on Flickr
Eight workshops operate today and they use traditional patterns but also experiment with modern ones
More information about kekfesto www.kekfestocotton.co.uk
This indigo print or blue print is believed to have come to Europe from the far East. The fabrics are hand printed in small quantities using wax printing blocks and then dipped in the indigo dye. The wax resists the dye and when removed the white pattern remains. Today there are only a handful of individuals still printing this fabric in this way.

Aboriginal

Fabric with Aboriginal painting pattern. Generally the symbols are a variation of lines, circles or dots.

Similar symbols can have multiple meanings and the elaborate combination of these can tell these complex Dreamtime stories.
Pirate Technics on Flickr
Aboriginal people have always worked with textiles. In communities all over Australia the weaving of native grasses and fibers has been an important part of the traditional economy. Aboriginal people used symbols to indicate a sacred site, the location of a water hole and the means to get there, a place where animals inhabit and as a way to illustrate dreamtime stories
